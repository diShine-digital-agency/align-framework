# Glossary

This glossary defines key terms used across the ALIGN framework wiki. Each entry includes a brief definition and a link to the page where the concept is discussed in depth. Terms are organized alphabetically.

For the full list of sources and citations, see [References and further reading](References-and-further-reading).

---

## A

**A/B testing (narrative)**
A method of comparing two versions of a content asset — differing in a specific narrative element such as villain framing, transformation language, or guide positioning — to determine which produces better engagement or conversion outcomes. See [Measuring narrative impact](Measuring-narrative-impact).

**ALIGN**
An acronym for the five phases of the ALIGN framework: **A**ssess, **L**ink, **I**ntegrate, **G**enerate, **N**avigate. The framework provides a structured methodology for developing and managing strategic narratives in B2B contexts. See [The ALIGN framework](The-ALIGN-framework).

**ALIGN Navigator**
An interactive tool that guides users through each phase of the ALIGN framework, providing prompts, templates, and decision support. See [ALIGN Navigator guide](ALIGN-Navigator-guide).

**Attribution model**
A method for assigning credit to marketing touchpoints that contributed to a conversion or sale. Common models include first-touch, multi-touch, and self-reported attribution. See [Measuring narrative impact](Measuring-narrative-impact).

---

## B

**Before state (narrative)**
The condition of the hero (customer) prior to the transformation that the guide (organization) facilitates. Defined during the narrative architecture phase to create contrast with the "after state." See [B2B narrative architecture](B2B-narrative-architecture).

**B2B narrative**
A strategic story designed for business-to-business contexts, where purchase decisions involve multiple stakeholders, long sales cycles, and rational-emotional evaluation. See [What is strategic storytelling](What-is-strategic-storytelling).

**Brand positioning**
The distinct place a brand occupies in the minds of its target audience relative to competitors. Narrative-driven brand positioning uses story structure rather than feature comparison to define this position. See [Brand positioning through narrative](Brand-positioning-through-narrative).

**Buyer journey**
The sequence of stages a prospective customer moves through — from awareness to consideration to decision — when evaluating a purchase. Strategic storytelling adapts narrative elements to each stage. See [Storytelling across the buyer journey](Storytelling-across-the-buyer-journey).

---

## C

**C2PA (Coalition for Content Provenance and Authenticity)**
An open technical standard for embedding provenance information in digital content, enabling verification of how content was created, including whether AI was involved. Developed by Adobe, Microsoft, Google, and other organizations. See [AI-generated content disclosure and ethics](AI-generated-content-disclosure-and-ethics).

**CAC (Customer Acquisition Cost)**
Total sales and marketing expenditure divided by the number of new customers acquired in a given period. A Tier 3 metric in the ALIGN KPI framework. See [Measuring narrative impact](Measuring-narrative-impact).

**Cognitive bias**
A systematic pattern of deviation from rational judgment. Relevant biases in B2B decision-making include loss aversion, status quo bias, the anchoring effect, and decision fatigue. See [Behavioral economics in B2B decisions](Behavioral-economics-in-B2B-decisions).

**Content Coherence Score**
A quarterly audit score derived from the Narrative Coherence Scorecard, measuring the alignment and consistency of an organization's published content against its Strategic Narrative Blueprint™. A Tier 1 metric in the ALIGN KPI framework; target is >80 out of 100. See [Measuring narrative impact](Measuring-narrative-impact).

**Content Narrative Matrix**
A planning tool that maps narrative elements — villain, transformation, proof points — to specific content formats and buyer journey stages, ensuring consistent story structure across all assets. See [Content strategy and narrative consistency](Content-strategy-and-narrative-consistency).

**Competitive differentiation**
The process of distinguishing an organization's offering from those of competitors. Narrative-driven differentiation focuses on framing the problem and transformation differently, rather than comparing feature sets. See [Brand positioning through narrative](Brand-positioning-through-narrative).

**Corporate communications**
The organizational function responsible for managing messaging to internal and external stakeholders. Strategic storytelling aligns corporate communications around a shared narrative architecture. See [Corporate communications and narrative strategy](Corporate-communications-and-narrative-strategy).

---

## D

**Decision fatigue**
The deterioration of decision quality after a long session of decision-making. B2B buying groups often experience decision fatigue, which creates a bias toward inaction or the status quo. See [Behavioral economics in B2B decisions](Behavioral-economics-in-B2B-decisions).

**Disclosure (AI content)**
The practice of informing audiences when content has been created or substantially assisted by artificial intelligence. Requirements vary by jurisdiction, platform, and content type. See [AI-generated content disclosure and ethics](AI-generated-content-disclosure-and-ethics).

---

## E

**Emotional resonance**
The degree to which a narrative activates an emotional response in the audience. Neuroscience research shows that emotionally resonant narratives produce stronger memory encoding and greater influence on decision-making. See [The neuroscience of strategic storytelling](The-neuroscience-of-strategic-storytelling).

**EU AI Act (Regulation 2024/1689)**
The European Union's comprehensive legal framework for artificial intelligence, adopted June 2024. Establishes risk-based obligations for AI providers and deployers, including transparency requirements for AI-generated content. See [EU AI Act and strategic storytelling](EU-AI-Act-and-strategic-storytelling).

---

## F

**fMRI (functional magnetic resonance imaging)**
A neuroimaging technique that measures brain activity by detecting changes in blood flow. Used in research to study how narratives activate brain regions including the prefrontal cortex, insula, and precuneus. See [The neuroscience of strategic storytelling](The-neuroscience-of-strategic-storytelling).

**Framework selection**
The process of choosing the most appropriate storytelling framework — such as StoryBrand, the Hero's Journey, PAS, or the Pixar Pitch — for a given strategic context. See [Storytelling frameworks compared](Storytelling-frameworks-compared).

**FTC (Federal Trade Commission)**
The US federal agency responsible for consumer protection, including truth-in-advertising enforcement. The FTC's Endorsement Guides and enforcement actions address AI-generated content, fake reviews, and disclosure requirements. See [AI-generated content disclosure and ethics](AI-generated-content-disclosure-and-ethics).

---

## G

**GPAI (General-Purpose AI)**
As defined by the EU AI Act, an AI model trained with a large amount of data using self-supervision at scale, that displays significant generality and is capable of competently performing a wide range of distinct tasks. Includes large language models like GPT and Claude. See [EU AI Act and strategic storytelling](EU-AI-Act-and-strategic-storytelling).

**Guide (narrative role)**
In story structure, the character who helps the hero overcome the challenge. In B2B storytelling, the organization positions itself as the guide — offering expertise, methodology, and tools — while the customer is the hero. See [The ALIGN framework](The-ALIGN-framework) and [Storytelling frameworks compared](Storytelling-frameworks-compared).

---

## H

**Hero (narrative role)**
In story structure, the central character who faces a challenge and undergoes transformation. In B2B storytelling, the customer is the hero. The organization's role is to serve as the guide. See [The ALIGN framework](The-ALIGN-framework).

**Hero's Journey**
A narrative structure identified by Joseph Campbell (1949) describing a hero who ventures into the unknown, faces trials, and returns transformed. Adapted for B2B contexts, it maps to the customer's journey from problem recognition through solution adoption. See [Storytelling frameworks compared](Storytelling-frameworks-compared).

---

## I

**Insula**
A region of the cerebral cortex involved in processing emotions including empathy, disgust, and risk assessment. Activated during narrative sequences that describe pain points or threats. See [The neuroscience of strategic storytelling](The-neuroscience-of-strategic-storytelling).

**Internal narrative alignment**
The degree to which all internal stakeholders — leadership, sales, marketing, customer success, product — share and can articulate the same core story. A prerequisite for external narrative consistency. See [Internal narrative alignment](Internal-narrative-alignment).

---

## J

**JTBD (Jobs to Be Done)**
A framework originated by Clayton Christensen that focuses on the "job" a customer is trying to accomplish rather than the product category. Useful for defining the hero's challenge in narrative architecture. See [Storytelling frameworks compared](Storytelling-frameworks-compared).

---

## K

**KPI dashboard (narrative)**
A measurement tool that tracks the nine ALIGN KPI metrics organized in three tiers — narrative consistency, engagement and resonance, and business outcomes. See [Measuring narrative impact](Measuring-narrative-impact).

---

## L

**Loss aversion**
The cognitive bias, documented by Tversky and Kahneman (1991), whereby the pain of losing something is psychologically approximately twice as intense as the pleasure of gaining something of equal value. In B2B storytelling, loss aversion is engaged through villain framing — illustrating what the hero stands to lose by not acting. See [Behavioral economics in B2B decisions](Behavioral-economics-in-B2B-decisions).

**Leading indicator**
A metric that predicts future outcomes. In the ALIGN KPI framework, Tier 1 metrics (Message Pull-Through Rate, Content Coherence Score, Narrative Adoption Rate) are leading indicators of future engagement and business results. See [Measuring narrative impact](Measuring-narrative-impact).

**Lagging indicator**
A metric that reflects past outcomes. In the ALIGN KPI framework, Tier 3 metrics (Sales Cycle Velocity, Win Rate, CAC) are lagging indicators that confirm whether narrative strategy is producing business results. See [Measuring narrative impact](Measuring-narrative-impact).

---

## M

**Message hierarchy**
A structured document that organizes the organization's key messages from strategic narrative (top level) to supporting proof points (bottom level). Ensures that all communications reinforce the core story. See [B2B narrative architecture](B2B-narrative-architecture).

**Message Pull-Through Rate**
The percentage of sales conversations that use the approved narrative structure. A Tier 1 metric in the ALIGN KPI framework; target is >70%. See [Measuring narrative impact](Measuring-narrative-impact).

**MQL (Marketing Qualified Lead)**
A prospect who has been identified as more likely to become a customer based on engagement with marketing content and activities. MQL-to-close time is a component of Sales Cycle Velocity measurement. See [Measuring narrative impact](Measuring-narrative-impact).

---

## N

**Narrative architecture**
The structural design of an organization's strategic story, including the defined villain, the hero, the guide, the transformation, and the proof points. Documented in the Strategic Narrative Blueprint™. See [B2B narrative architecture](B2B-narrative-architecture).

**Narrative audit**
A systematic assessment of an organization's existing content and messaging to identify inconsistencies, gaps, and opportunities for narrative alignment. Conducted during Phase 1 (Assess) of the ALIGN framework. See [The narrative audit methodology](The-narrative-audit-methodology).

**Narrative Coherence Score™**
A proprietary scoring methodology used in the ALIGN framework to quantify the degree of narrative consistency across an organization's content ecosystem. Assessed through the Narrative Coherence Scorecard during the narrative audit. See [The narrative audit methodology](The-narrative-audit-methodology).

**Narrative coupling**
The phenomenon, documented by Hasson et al. (2010), where a speaker's brain activity patterns are mirrored in the listener's brain during storytelling. Stronger coupling correlates with greater comprehension and recall. See [The neuroscience of strategic storytelling](The-neuroscience-of-strategic-storytelling).

**Neural coupling**
See: Narrative coupling.

**NRR (Net Revenue Retention)**
The percentage of recurring revenue retained from existing customers over a period, including expansions and contractions. An indicator of whether the narrative resonates post-sale and supports customer retention. See [Measuring narrative impact](Measuring-narrative-impact).

---

## O

**Oxytocin**
A neuropeptide associated with empathy, trust, and social bonding. Research by Zak (2014) demonstrated that character-driven stories with emotional tension increase oxytocin levels, which correlates with greater willingness to cooperate and share. See [The neuroscience of strategic storytelling](The-neuroscience-of-strategic-storytelling).

---

## P

**PAS (Problem-Agitate-Solution)**
A copywriting and storytelling framework that identifies a problem, amplifies the emotional impact of that problem, and then presents a solution. One of several frameworks compared in the ALIGN framework context. See [Storytelling frameworks compared](Storytelling-frameworks-compared).

**Pixar Pitch**
A storytelling structure derived from Pixar's narrative formula: "Once upon a time... Every day... One day... Because of that... Because of that... Until finally..." Useful for distilling complex narratives into concise, compelling summaries. See [Storytelling frameworks compared](Storytelling-frameworks-compared).

**Precuneus**
A brain region involved in self-referential processing and autobiographical memory. Activated when audiences relate a narrative to their own experiences — a key mechanism in narrative persuasion. See [The neuroscience of strategic storytelling](The-neuroscience-of-strategic-storytelling).

---

## R

**Risk classification (AI Act)**
The EU AI Act's four-tier system for categorizing AI applications: unacceptable risk (prohibited), high risk (conformity assessment required), limited risk (transparency obligations), and minimal risk (voluntary codes). See [EU AI Act and strategic storytelling](EU-AI-Act-and-strategic-storytelling).

---

## S

**Sales cycle velocity**
The speed at which prospects move from initial qualification (MQL) to closed deal. A Tier 3 metric in the ALIGN KPI framework; target is 15–20% reduction within six months of narrative deployment. See [Measuring narrative impact](Measuring-narrative-impact).

**Self-referential encoding**
A memory phenomenon documented by Rogers, Kuiper, and Kirker (1977) showing that information processed in relation to the self is remembered better than information processed in other ways. In storytelling, narratives that invite the audience to see themselves in the hero's situation activate this encoding. See [The neuroscience of strategic storytelling](The-neuroscience-of-strategic-storytelling).

**Status quo bias**
The preference for the current state of affairs, documented by Samuelson and Zeckhauser (1988). In B2B contexts, status quo bias is one of the primary obstacles to purchase decisions. Effective villain framing helps overcome this bias by making the cost of inaction vivid. See [Behavioral economics in B2B decisions](Behavioral-economics-in-B2B-decisions).

**StoryBrand**
A storytelling framework developed by Donald Miller (2017) that positions the customer as the hero and the brand as the guide. The ALIGN framework draws on StoryBrand principles while extending them with neuroscience, behavioral economics, and B2B-specific methodology. See [Storytelling frameworks compared](Storytelling-frameworks-compared).

**Strategic Narrative Blueprint™**
The central deliverable of the ALIGN framework — a documented narrative architecture that defines the villain, hero, guide, transformation, proof points, and messaging hierarchy for an organization. See [B2B narrative architecture](B2B-narrative-architecture).

**Synthetic content**
Content generated or substantially modified by an AI system. Under the EU AI Act, synthetic content requires machine-readable labeling to indicate its artificial origin. See [EU AI Act and strategic storytelling](EU-AI-Act-and-strategic-storytelling) and [AI-generated content disclosure and ethics](AI-generated-content-disclosure-and-ethics).

---

## T

**Transportation (narrative)**
A psychological state described by Green and Brock (2000) in which an audience member becomes absorbed in a story, reducing critical resistance and increasing the narrative's influence on beliefs and attitudes. Transportation is the primary mechanism through which strategic storytelling produces attitude change. See [The neuroscience of strategic storytelling](The-neuroscience-of-strategic-storytelling).

**Transparency obligation**
A legal requirement under the EU AI Act (Article 50) for providers and deployers of certain AI systems to inform users about AI involvement. Applies to chatbots, synthetic content, and deep fakes. See [EU AI Act and strategic storytelling](EU-AI-Act-and-strategic-storytelling).

---

## U

**Unique mechanism**
The specific process, methodology, or approach that distinguishes an organization's solution from competitors. In narrative architecture, the unique mechanism is the "how" that makes the guide's approach credible. See [B2B narrative architecture](B2B-narrative-architecture).

---

## V

**Villain (narrative role)**
In story structure, the antagonist that creates the hero's challenge. In B2B storytelling, the villain is typically a problem, inefficiency, outdated approach, or market force — not a competitor. Effective villain framing makes the cost of inaction concrete and emotionally resonant. See [The ALIGN framework](The-ALIGN-framework) and [B2B narrative architecture](B2B-narrative-architecture).

---

## W

**Win rate**
The percentage of opportunities that result in a closed-won deal, typically measured for competitive deals (where at least one named competitor was evaluated). A Tier 3 metric in the ALIGN KPI framework; target is >35% in competitive situations. See [Measuring narrative impact](Measuring-narrative-impact).
