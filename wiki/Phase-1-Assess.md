# Phase 1: Assess — narrative ecosystem mapping

Phase 1 is the diagnostic foundation of the [ALIGN framework](The-ALIGN-framework). Before building a new narrative, you must objectively measure how broken the current one is. This phase moves the conversation from subjective opinions ("I think our messaging is fine") to quantified data ("Our Narrative Coherence Score is 47 out of 100, with the largest gap in emotional resonance").

The Assess phase has two steps:

1. **Step 1.1: The internal audit** — score your current narrative across four dimensions using the Narrative Coherence Scorecard™.
2. **Step 1.2: The external validation** — test your internal findings against real customer perception through structured interviews.

Neither step alone is sufficient. The internal audit tells you what you think your narrative is. The external validation tells you what your customers actually experience. The gap between these two is where the real diagnostic value lies.

---

## Purpose

Most B2B companies believe their narrative is stronger than it actually is. Internal teams are too close to the product to evaluate it objectively (see [The Curse of Knowledge](#the-curse-of-knowledge) below). Sales teams develop their own messaging variants. Marketing produces content that drifts from the core story over time. The result is narrative fragmentation — a condition where no two touchpoints tell the same story, and the customer receives a confused, inconsistent signal.

Phase 1 exists to make this fragmentation visible and measurable. It produces a single quantitative output — the **Narrative Coherence Score™** — that serves as the baseline for every subsequent phase. Without this baseline, [Phase 2: Link](Phase-2-Link) has no starting point, and there is no way to measure whether the work you do in later phases actually improved anything.

## Step 1.1: The internal audit

The internal audit uses the **Narrative Coherence Scorecard™**, a structured assessment that produces a score from 0 to 100 across four equally weighted dimensions. The full template is available in the repository: [01-assess-narrative-scorecard.md](https://github.com/diShine-digital-agency/align-framework/blob/main/templates/01-assess-narrative-scorecard.md).

### Materials to gather

Before scoring, collect the following materials. These represent the touchpoints where your narrative is most visible to prospects:

- Website homepage.
- About page.
- Primary sales deck (the one your sales team actually uses, not the aspirational version in the brand folder).
- 3 recent case studies.
- 3 recent email campaigns (ideally a mix of nurture, outbound, and product announcement).

### The four dimensions

Each dimension is scored from 0 to 25 points (five criteria at 0–5 each). The four dimensions together produce the total Narrative Coherence Score™ (0–100).

#### Narrative consistency (0–25)

Does the story hold together across all touchpoints, or does it fragment depending on who is speaking and where?

This dimension evaluates whether the core value proposition, the villain (the problem you solve), the hero (the customer), tone of voice, and visual narrative remain consistent from homepage to sales deck to email campaign. A score of 25 means a prospect could encounter your brand on any channel and receive the same fundamental story. A score near zero means every touchpoint tells a different story.

#### Message hierarchy (0–25)

Is the story structured **Why → How → What**, or does it lead with features?

This dimension is grounded in the principle that effective B2B narratives establish purpose and transformation before introducing capabilities. It evaluates whether the overarching "why" (the transformation you enable) appears before the "how" (your unique mechanism or methodology), and whether features are presented as proof points supporting the mechanism — not as the main event. It also checks for jargon, internal acronyms, and whether the narrative can be understood by scanning headlines alone.

#### Emotional resonance (0–25)

Does the story connect with the psychological drivers of B2B buyers, or is it purely rational and functional?

B2B purchase decisions are not purely rational. They involve career risk, internal politics, fear of making the wrong choice, and aspiration for professional advancement. This dimension evaluates whether your messaging demonstrates empathy with the buyer's daily frustrations, paints a compelling "after state," articulates the cost of inaction, uses case studies that highlight human transformation (not just ROI tables), and feels authentic rather than hyperbolic.

For more on the psychological drivers behind B2B decisions, see [Behavioral economics in B2B decisions](Behavioral-economics-in-B2B-decisions).

#### Competitive differentiation (0–25)

Does the story carve out a unique market position, or could it be copied onto a competitor's website without anyone noticing?

This dimension evaluates whether you take a clear stance against a specific industry status quo, articulate a paradigm shift that makes your approach necessary, present a narrative that cannot be trivially replicated by competitors, back your story with unique data or a proprietary methodology, and keep the core narrative simple enough for a prospect to repeat it to their internal buying committee.

### Score interpretation

| Score range | Classification | What it means |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **80–100** | Strategic alignment | Your narrative is an effective asset driving revenue. Focus on [Phase 5: Navigate](Phase-5-Navigate) to optimize and scale. |
| **60–79** | Tactical fragmentation | You have strong story elements, but they are not connected strategically. Focus on [Phase 3: Integrate](Phase-3-Integrate) to build a unified architecture. |
| **40–59** | Feature-led communication | You are competing on price and features rather than value. Focus heavily on [Phase 2: Link](Phase-2-Link) to build a Strategic Narrative Blueprint. |
| **0–39** | Narrative disconnect | Your messaging is confusing the market and hindering growth. A complete narrative rebuild is required, starting from Phase 1. |

### Owner

The internal audit should be owned by the **Head of Marketing** or, in early-stage companies, the **Founder**. The scorer must have access to all current marketing and sales materials and must be willing to score honestly. If your Head of Marketing scores every dimension a 5, the audit has failed before it started.

## Step 1.2: The external validation

The internal audit tells you what you think your narrative is. Step 1.2 tells you what your customers actually experienced. This step uses the **Narrative Audit Interview Guide**, a 20-question structured interview designed to uncover the real cognitive and emotional drivers behind purchase decisions. The full guide is available in the repository: [narrative-audit-interview-guide.md](https://github.com/diShine-digital-agency/align-framework/blob/main/templates/narrative-audit-interview-guide.md).

For an in-depth analysis of the methodology and psychology behind this interview, see [The narrative audit methodology](The-narrative-audit-methodology).

### Target participants

- **5–7 recent wins** — customers who closed within the last 90 days. This recency constraint is critical: memory of the buying journey degrades quickly, and customers who bought 6–12 months ago will rationalize their decision differently than those who bought recently.
- **3–5 recent losses** — prospects who evaluated your solution but chose a competitor or chose to do nothing. Losses are often more diagnostically valuable than wins because they reveal where the narrative failed to overcome objections, fear, or inertia.

Do not interview legacy customers who bought years ago. Their buying context is outdated, and their memory of the decision process has been overwritten by their usage experience.

### Why standard surveys are insufficient

Standard customer satisfaction surveys and NPS scores measure satisfaction with the product, not the effectiveness of the narrative that drove the purchase. Asking "Why did you buy?" in a survey yields post-hoc rationalizations, not the real decision drivers.

The Narrative Audit Interview is designed to bypass **post-purchase rationalization** (choice-supportive bias) — the well-documented tendency for buyers to justify their decisions with logical reasons after the fact, even when the actual decision was driven by emotional or contextual factors. The interview achieves this through careful question sequencing that takes the interviewee back to specific moments in their buying journey, before the purchase was made, when uncertainty was still high.

### The "Five Whys" rule

Never accept the first functional answer. If a customer says "We bought because of the API," that is a feature-level answer. The real narrative insight is buried beneath it:

- *Why did the API matter?* "Because our old tool couldn't integrate with Salesforce."
- *Why did Salesforce integration matter?* "Because our sales team was manually re-entering data."
- *Why was that a problem?* "Because we were losing 4 hours per rep per week."
- *Why did that matter to you personally?* "Because I was the one who had to explain to the VP of Sales why our CRM data was always out of date."
- *Why was that significant?* "Because it was making me look incompetent in front of leadership."

The fifth answer — career risk, personal reputation, internal politics — is the narrative insight. The first answer — "the API" — is a feature. Your narrative should speak to the fifth answer, not the first.

### The four sections of the interview

The 20 questions are organized into four sections that mirror the stages of a B2B buying journey. Each section maps directly to elements of the Strategic Narrative Blueprint produced in [Phase 2: Link](Phase-2-Link).

#### Section 1: Status quo and trigger (questions 1–5)

Objective: understand the exact conditions that forced the buyer out of inertia. What was the "before state"? What was the true cost of doing nothing?

These questions uncover the opening hook of your narrative — the inciting incident that started the buying journey. They also reveal your true competitor, which is often not another vendor but an internal workaround (spreadsheets, manual processes, or an intern).

#### Section 2: Search and villain (questions 6–10)

Objective: understand how the buyer framed the problem in their own mind and what they perceived as the root cause.

These questions define the "villain" of your narrative — not a competitor by name, but the outdated methodology, flawed assumption, or structural limitation that your competitors embody. They also capture the buyer's actual search vocabulary (critical for top-of-funnel messaging) and reveal the fears and risks that almost prevented the purchase.

#### Section 3: Guide and unique mechanism (questions 11–15)

Objective: validate your brand positioning and the perceived value of your differentiated approach.

These questions test whether your narrative is actually landing. Question 13 — "How would you describe what we do to a colleague in one sentence?" — is the ultimate test of narrative clarity. If five customers give five completely different answers, your narrative is fragmented regardless of what your internal scorecard says.

#### Section 4: Transformation and after state (questions 16–20)

Objective: quantify the functional and emotional transformation to build the "after state" of your narrative.

These questions capture the external success (measurable metrics), internal success (personal impact on the buyer's role and reputation), and philosophical success (the new reality you created for them). Question 19 — "If you left your company and went to a competitor, would you bring us with you?" — tests narrative loyalty at the deepest level.

### Owner

The external validation should be owned by **Product Marketing** or an **external consultant**. The interviewer should not be someone from the sales team who has an existing relationship with the interviewee, as this introduces social desirability bias — the tendency for interviewees to give answers they believe the interviewer wants to hear.

## Why this phase matters: the psychology

Four cognitive biases make Phase 1 necessary. Without a structured diagnostic process, these biases cause internal teams to overestimate narrative quality and misunderstand customer perception.

For a comprehensive treatment of these principles and their application across the full framework, see [Behavioral economics in B2B decisions](Behavioral-economics-in-B2B-decisions).

### Post-purchase rationalization (choice-supportive bias)

After making a purchase decision, buyers retroactively attribute the decision to rational, logical factors — even when the actual decision was driven by emotional triggers, social proof, or risk aversion. This means that asking customers "Why did you buy?" after the fact produces a distorted picture of the real decision drivers.

The Narrative Audit Interview Guide is specifically designed to circumvent this bias by taking interviewees back to specific moments before the decision was made, when uncertainty was still present and emotions were still active.

### The Curse of Knowledge

Once you know something, you cannot imagine not knowing it. Internal teams have deep product knowledge, which makes them structurally incapable of evaluating whether their narrative is clear to someone encountering it for the first time.

The Curse of Knowledge explains why companies overuse jargon, skip foundational context, and lead with technical capabilities instead of the transformation they enable. The internal audit (Step 1.1) surfaces this gap by forcing a structured evaluation of message hierarchy. The external validation (Step 1.2) confirms it by testing real customer comprehension.

### Status Quo Bias

People prefer the current state of affairs, even when the current state is objectively suboptimal. In a B2B context, this means your biggest competitor is often not another vendor — it is the prospect's decision to do nothing.

Effective narratives must overcome Status Quo Bias by articulating a clear cost of inaction. The Narrative Audit Interview (Section 1, questions 4–5) specifically probes whether buyers perceived a compelling cost of staying with their current process.

### Loss Aversion

The pain of losing something is psychologically approximately twice as intense as the pleasure of gaining something of equivalent value. This principle, established by Kahneman and Tversky, has direct implications for B2B narrative design.

Narratives that frame the value proposition in terms of what the buyer stands to lose by not acting are psychologically more compelling than those framing equivalent gains. Phase 1 evaluates whether the current narrative leverages this asymmetry or defaults to gain-framed messaging ("You will gain efficiency") instead of loss-framed messaging ("You are losing 4 hours per rep per week").

## Outputs of Phase 1

Phase 1 produces the following deliverables:

- **Narrative Coherence Score™ (0–100)** — the quantitative baseline, scored across four dimensions (narrative consistency, message hierarchy, emotional resonance, competitive differentiation). This score is the starting measurement against which all future narrative work is evaluated.
- **Customer validation interview findings** — synthesized themes from 8–12 structured interviews (wins and losses), mapped to the four sections of the interview guide. These findings either confirm or contradict the internal audit scores.
- **Gap analysis** — a documented comparison of the internal audit results against the external validation findings. Where the internal score is high but customer interviews tell a different story, the gap analysis identifies the specific disconnect.

These outputs feed directly into [Phase 2: Link](Phase-2-Link), where the Strategic Narrative Blueprint™ is constructed. Without the Phase 1 baseline, Phase 2 has no empirical foundation — it becomes an exercise in opinion rather than strategy.

## Common mistakes

### Skipping customer interviews

The most frequent and most damaging mistake. Teams complete the internal scorecard, feel confident in their score, and move directly to Phase 2. This defeats the purpose of the Assess phase. The internal audit measures what you think your narrative is. Without external validation, you have no way of knowing whether that perception matches market reality. In practice, the gap between internal perception and external experience is almost always larger than expected.

### Scoring too generously

Internal teams consistently overrate their own narrative quality. This is not dishonesty; it is a predictable consequence of the Curse of Knowledge and confirmation bias. The person scoring the audit has been immersed in the company's messaging for months or years and cannot evaluate it with fresh eyes.

Countermeasure: have someone outside the core marketing team (a new hire, an advisor, or an external consultant) independently complete the scorecard using the same materials. Compare scores. If the two scores differ by more than 15 points, the higher score is almost certainly inflated.

### Auditing only one channel

Scoring only the website while ignoring the sales deck, email campaigns, and case studies produces an incomplete picture. Narrative fragmentation is a cross-channel phenomenon. A company may have a strong homepage story that completely breaks down in sales decks written by individual reps. Phase 1 requires gathering materials from all primary touchpoints as listed in the [scorecard template](https://github.com/diShine-digital-agency/align-framework/blob/main/templates/01-assess-narrative-scorecard.md).
