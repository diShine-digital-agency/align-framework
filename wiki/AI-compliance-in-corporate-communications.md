# AI compliance in corporate communications

## Introduction

As organizations increasingly adopt AI tools — large language models, image generators, content automation platforms — for corporate communications, a patchwork of regulations and guidelines has emerged across jurisdictions. These rules aim to protect consumers, ensure transparency, and maintain trust in public-facing content.

This page provides an overview of the regulatory landscape relevant to marketing, content strategy, and corporate storytelling. It is intended as a practical reference for communications professionals, content strategists, and compliance teams who need to understand where AI-generated content intersects with legal and ethical obligations.

The regulatory environment is evolving rapidly. As of early 2026, some frameworks (such as the EU AI Act) are in phased implementation, while others (such as US federal AI legislation) remain largely principles-based or enforced through existing consumer protection statutes. This page reflects the state of the law at the time of writing and should not be treated as a substitute for legal counsel.

## Why AI compliance matters for strategic storytelling

The ALIGN framework is methodology-driven and does not prescribe specific AI tools. It provides a structured process — Assess, Link, Integrate, Generate, Navigate — for developing strategic narratives. However, many teams implementing the framework will use AI assistance at various stages: narrative assessment and scoring (Phase 1: Assess), strategic-narrative alignment (Phase 2: Link), framework selection and architecture (Phase 3: Integrate), content ecosystem development (Phase 4: Generate), or implementation and measurement (Phase 5: Navigate).

When AI tools are used in any of these phases, the resulting content may trigger regulatory obligations. These obligations exist regardless of which AI tool is used or how extensively it is employed. A blog post drafted by a large language model and then edited by a human may still require disclosure. A social media image created with a generative AI tool may need labeling. A chatbot interacting with customers on a corporate website almost certainly requires transparency measures.

Understanding these obligations is not optional. Regulatory enforcement is increasing, penalties can be substantial, and reputational damage from non-compliance can undermine the very trust that strategic storytelling is designed to build.

## Key regulatory frameworks

Three broad jurisdictions shape the current AI compliance landscape for corporate communications. Each takes a different approach, but they share common themes: transparency, accuracy, and consumer protection.

### EU AI Act (Regulation (EU) 2024/1689)

The EU AI Act, formally adopted in June 2024 and entered into force in August 2024, is the first comprehensive AI-specific regulation worldwide. It establishes a risk-based classification system for AI applications and imposes graduated obligations based on the level of risk an AI system poses.

For corporate communications, the most relevant provisions are:

- **Transparency obligations**: Users must be informed when they are interacting with an AI system (such as a chatbot). Content that is AI-generated — including text, images, audio, and video — must be labeled as such when it is made publicly available.
- **Synthetic content labeling**: AI-generated or manipulated content (sometimes called "deepfakes" or "synthetic media") must be disclosed. This applies to marketing materials, social media content, and any publicly distributed communications.
- **General-purpose AI (GPAI) model requirements**: Providers of GPAI models (the companies building large language models, for instance) have documentation and transparency obligations. Organizations that deploy these models downstream — including for content generation — must ensure their use complies with the Act's requirements.
- **AI literacy**: Article 4 requires organizations deploying AI systems to ensure that their staff have sufficient understanding of AI capabilities, limitations, and applicable regulations.

The EU AI Act is being implemented in phases, with most provisions applying between 2025 and 2027. For a detailed breakdown, see [EU AI Act and strategic storytelling](EU-AI-Act-and-strategic-storytelling).

### United States (FTC guidelines and state laws)

The United States does not have a single comprehensive federal AI law comparable to the EU AI Act as of early 2026. Instead, AI-related obligations arise from a combination of existing consumer protection statutes, Federal Trade Commission (FTC) enforcement actions, and an increasing number of state-level laws.

Key elements include:

- **FTC enforcement**: The FTC has stated repeatedly that existing truth-in-advertising laws apply fully to AI-generated content. There is "no AI exemption" from requirements that advertising be truthful, non-deceptive, and substantiated. The FTC has brought enforcement actions against companies for deceptive AI-related practices, including undisclosed AI-generated reviews and misleading claims about AI capabilities.
- **Disclosure requirements**: The FTC expects clear and conspicuous disclosure when content is AI-generated, particularly for testimonials, endorsements, and advertising. Disclosures must be placed where consumers will actually see them — not buried in footnotes or terms of service.
- **State laws**: Several states have enacted or proposed AI-specific legislation. California's AI Transparency Act (SB 942), signed into law in 2024, requires providers of generative AI systems to offer AI content detection tools and to include both overt and latent disclosures in AI-generated content. Other states have enacted laws requiring chatbot disclosure or AI labeling in political advertising.

For more detail on US disclosure requirements, see [AI-generated content disclosure and ethics](AI-generated-content-disclosure-and-ethics).

### Other jurisdictions

- **United Kingdom**: The UK has adopted a sector-specific, principles-based approach to AI regulation rather than comprehensive legislation. Existing regulators (such as the Advertising Standards Authority, the Competition and Markets Authority, and the Information Commissioner's Office) are expected to apply existing rules to AI within their domains. The UK government published an AI regulation white paper in 2023 outlining five cross-sector principles: safety, transparency, fairness, accountability, and contestability.
- **Canada**: Canada's Artificial Intelligence and Data Act (AIDA), introduced as Part 3 of Bill C-27, was proposed to regulate high-impact AI systems. As of early 2026, the legislation has not been enacted. Canada's existing competition and advertising laws apply to AI-generated commercial content.
- **China**: China has implemented several AI-specific regulations, including rules on generative AI services (effective August 2023) that require labeling of AI-generated content, prohibit generating content that undermines state security, and require providers to register algorithms with authorities. These regulations are primarily relevant to organizations operating within or targeting Chinese markets.

## Core obligations across jurisdictions

Despite differences in approach, several common obligations emerge across regulatory frameworks. Communications teams should treat these as baseline requirements.

### Transparency

Inform users when content is AI-generated or when they are interacting with an AI system. This is an explicit requirement under the EU AI Act and an implicit requirement under FTC guidance on deceptive practices. Transparency is the single most consistent theme across jurisdictions.

### Accuracy

AI-generated claims must be truthful and substantiated. Large language models can produce plausible-sounding but factually incorrect content (commonly referred to as "hallucinations"). Under FTC standards, a company is responsible for the accuracy of its advertising and marketing content regardless of whether that content was written by a human or generated by an AI tool. The EU AI Act's transparency and documentation requirements serve a similar purpose.

### Disclosure

Clearly label AI-generated or AI-assisted content, especially in advertising, endorsements, and testimonials. Disclosure requirements vary by jurisdiction and context, but the general principle is that audiences should not be misled about the origin of content. Disclosures should be clear, conspicuous, and placed at the point of engagement — not hidden in metadata, fine print, or separate pages.

### Documentation

Maintain records of AI system usage, content review processes, and editorial decisions. The EU AI Act includes specific documentation requirements for certain categories of AI systems. Even where documentation is not explicitly required by law, maintaining audit trails is a practical risk-management measure that demonstrates good faith compliance efforts.

### Human oversight

Ensure meaningful human review of AI-generated content before publication. "Meaningful" review means a qualified person actually reads, evaluates, and takes responsibility for the content — not a perfunctory sign-off on machine output. Human oversight is both a regulatory expectation (particularly under the EU AI Act's requirements for high-risk systems) and a practical necessity given the current limitations of generative AI.

## Risk areas for B2B communicators

Certain categories of corporate communications present elevated compliance risk when AI tools are involved.

### AI-generated testimonials or case studies

Customer testimonials and case studies are a staple of B2B marketing. If these are generated, substantially drafted, or embellished by AI, disclosure is required. The FTC has been particularly active in enforcement around fake or misleading testimonials. Even if a real customer provided the underlying information, AI-generated elaboration or rewriting may trigger disclosure obligations.

### AI-generated social media content

Social media posts, comments, and engagement generated by AI tools must comply with platform-specific policies and applicable regulations. This includes both organic content and paid advertising. Several platforms now require labeling of AI-generated content, and failure to comply can result in content removal or account restrictions.

### Chatbots and virtual agents in customer-facing roles

AI-powered chatbots used for customer service, lead generation, or sales must disclose their non-human nature. This is an explicit requirement under the EU AI Act and increasingly expected under US state laws. The disclosure should occur at the beginning of the interaction, not after a user has already engaged.

### AI-generated product descriptions and marketing copy

Product descriptions, white papers, data sheets, and other marketing materials generated by AI must meet the same accuracy and substantiation standards as human-written content. AI-generated claims about product capabilities, performance, or results must be supported by evidence. This is particularly important in regulated industries such as financial services, healthcare, and technology.

## Best practices for AI-compliant content operations

The following practices can help communications teams manage compliance risk while still benefiting from AI tools.

### Establish an AI content policy

Create a written policy that defines how AI tools may be used in content creation, what types of content require disclosure, and who is responsible for compliance. The policy should be specific enough to guide day-to-day decisions and flexible enough to accommodate evolving regulations and tools.

### Implement human review workflows

Build human review into every content workflow where AI tools are used. Review should be substantive — checking facts, evaluating tone, verifying claims, and ensuring compliance with disclosure requirements. Assign clear responsibility for review and sign-off.

### Maintain audit trails

Keep records of which AI tools were used, what prompts or inputs were provided, what output was generated, and what edits were made by human reviewers. These records serve as evidence of compliance efforts and support internal accountability.

### Stay current with evolving regulations

AI regulation is changing rapidly. Designate a team member or function responsible for monitoring regulatory developments and updating internal policies accordingly. Subscribe to updates from relevant regulatory bodies (the EU AI Office, the FTC, relevant state legislatures) and industry associations.

### Train teams on obligations

Ensure that everyone involved in content creation — writers, editors, designers, marketers, strategists — understands the organization's AI content policy and the regulatory obligations that inform it. Training should be practical, role-specific, and updated regularly as regulations evolve. The EU AI Act's AI literacy requirement (Article 4) makes training an explicit legal obligation for organizations deploying AI within the EU.
