# Storytelling frameworks compared

No single storytelling framework serves all B2B communication needs. The most effective approach involves selecting — or combining — frameworks based on business objectives, audience sophistication, deal complexity, and go-to-market strategy. A framework that works for a cold email sequence will fail as the architecture for a 12-month enterprise sales narrative. A framework designed for internal alignment will collapse under the weight of a full content strategy.

This page provides an in-depth analysis of each major storytelling framework used in B2B contexts: its origins, its structural mechanics, its psychological basis, its strengths, its limitations, and its ideal application. The goal is not to crown a winner but to equip practitioners with the understanding necessary to make an informed selection — or, more commonly, to combine elements from multiple frameworks into a coherent narrative architecture.

For a condensed comparison, see the [framework comparison matrix](https://github.com/diShine-digital-agency/align-framework/blob/main/guides/framework-comparison-matrix.md) in the main repository. This page goes substantially deeper into the theory, psychology, and practical deployment considerations behind each framework.

## The Hero's Journey (Campbell/Vogler)

### Origin and development

The Hero's Journey is the most structurally comprehensive storytelling framework available. Its roots lie in comparative mythology. In 1949, Joseph Campbell published *The Hero with a Thousand Faces*, in which he identified a common narrative pattern — the "monomyth" — recurring across myths, religions, and folk tales from cultures separated by geography, language, and millennia. Campbell's argument was that these recurring patterns were not coincidental. They reflected deep psychological structures shared by all humans — what Carl Jung had earlier described as archetypes residing in the collective unconscious.

Campbell's original formulation described 17 stages organized into three acts: Departure, Initiation, and Return. The framework was dense, academic, and intended for literary analysis rather than practical application.

In 1992, Christopher Vogler — then a story analyst at Disney — published *The Writer's Journey: Mythic Structure for Writers*, in which he distilled Campbell's 17 stages into 12 and reframed them as a practical tool for screenwriters. Vogler's adaptation made the Hero's Journey accessible to practitioners. Nearly every major Hollywood film from *Star Wars* to *The Matrix* to *The Lion King* follows some version of Vogler's 12-stage structure.

The adaptation of the Hero's Journey to B2B marketing and sales narrative is more recent, but the underlying logic is sound: enterprise buying is a journey of transformation. The buyer begins in one state (the status quo), encounters a problem (the call to adventure), faces obstacles (internal politics, competing priorities, implementation risk), and — if the narrative is effective — emerges transformed (the return with the elixir). The framework provides a complete structural scaffold for this journey.

### The 12 stages in detail

Each of the 12 stages maps to a recognizable moment in the B2B buying process. Understanding these mappings is essential for deploying the Hero's Journey effectively.

**Stage 1: The ordinary world.** The narrative begins by establishing the buyer's current reality — the status quo. In B2B, this is the world before the problem becomes urgent. The buyer's team is using spreadsheets, legacy software, or manual processes. Things are functional but suboptimal. The ordinary world must be described with enough specificity that the buyer recognizes themselves in it. Generic descriptions ("companies struggle with inefficiency") fail because they do not trigger episodic memory retrieval. Specific descriptions ("your operations team spends 14 hours each week manually reconciling data between three systems that don't talk to each other") succeed because they activate recognition.

**Stage 2: The call to adventure.** Something disrupts the ordinary world. In B2B, this is typically a triggering event: a new executive mandate, a failed audit, a lost customer, a competitor's product launch, or a board-level demand for better metrics. The call to adventure is the moment the status quo becomes untenable. The narrative audit interview (question 1) is specifically designed to identify this moment in the buyer's own experience. The call must be external and concrete — not a vague feeling that "things could be better" but a specific event that forced action.

**Stage 3: Refusal of the call.** The hero initially resists the call. In B2B, this manifests as the organizational inertia that kills most deals. Status quo bias, loss aversion, and regret avoidance combine to make "do nothing" the default choice. CEB (now Gartner) research consistently finds that 40–60% of qualified B2B deals end in no decision. The refusal is not irrational — change is genuinely risky, costly, and disruptive. The narrative must acknowledge this resistance honestly rather than dismissing it. Buyers who feel their concerns are being trivialized will disengage.

**Stage 4: Meeting the mentor.** The hero encounters a guide who provides the tools, knowledge, or confidence to proceed. In B2B, the mentor is your brand — but critically, the brand must position itself as the *guide*, not the hero. The buyer is the hero. This distinction, which Donald Miller emphasizes heavily in the StoryBrand framework, is the most common mistake in B2B narrative: companies cast themselves as the hero of their own story, which leaves no role for the buyer. The mentor provides two things — empathy (demonstrating genuine understanding of the buyer's situation) and authority (demonstrating competence to help). These are validated in the narrative audit through questions 14 and 15.

**Stage 5: Crossing the threshold.** The hero commits to the journey. In B2B, this is the initial commitment — signing up for a pilot, agreeing to a proof of concept, or allocating budget for a formal evaluation. The threshold is psychologically significant because it marks the point of no return. The narrative must make crossing feel like progress rather than risk.

**Stage 6: Tests, allies, and enemies.** The hero faces trials, discovers allies, and encounters enemies. In complex B2B sales, this stage is often the longest and most treacherous. The "tests" are implementation challenges — technical integration, data migration, workflow redesign. The "allies" are internal champions, early adopters, and supportive executives. The "enemies" are detractors, competing priorities, budget constraints, and organizational politics. Content created for this stage includes implementation guides, change management resources, and internal-champion enablement materials.

**Stage 7: Approach to the inmost cave.** The hero prepares for the central challenge. In B2B, this is the preparation for the single biggest hurdle in the buying or implementation process — the board presentation, the technical integration, the go-live decision. The narrative must anticipate and address the specific anxieties associated with this moment.

**Stage 8: The ordeal.** The hero confronts the central crisis. In B2B, this is the make-or-break moment of implementation or adoption — the point where the project either succeeds or fails. The narrative's role here is to provide reassurance through evidence: case studies of companies that navigated similar ordeals, data on success rates, access to expert support.

**Stage 9: The reward.** The hero achieves initial success. In B2B, these are the early wins — the first report generated in minutes instead of hours, the first quarter with measurable ROI, the first positive feedback from end users. The narrative should help the buyer recognize and celebrate these wins, as they build the momentum necessary to sustain adoption.

**Stage 10: The road back.** The hero begins the return journey, integrating the reward into their world. In B2B, this is the transition from implementation to daily operations — making the solution part of the organizational fabric rather than a special project. Content for this stage includes best practices, optimization guides, and advanced training.

**Stage 11: The resurrection.** The hero faces a final test that proves the transformation is complete. In B2B, this might be a major stress test — a peak-season performance challenge, an audit, or an organizational restructuring that validates the new system's resilience. The narrative should prepare buyers for this test and celebrate their passage through it.

**Stage 12: Return with the elixir.** The hero returns transformed, bearing gifts for the community. In B2B, the "elixir" is the measurable transformation — the data, the efficiency gains, the strategic capabilities — that the buyer can share with their organization. The buyer becomes an advocate, providing testimonials, case studies, and referrals. The narrative cycle is complete, and the buyer's story becomes a tool for the next buyer's journey.

### Why the Hero's Journey works psychologically

The Hero's Journey works because it mirrors the psychological structure of how humans process change. Campbell argued, drawing on Jung, that the monomyth reflects universal psychological processes — the ego's confrontation with the unknown, the integration of shadow material, and the return to the conscious self with new capabilities. While the Jungian framework is debated, the structural observation is robust: humans across all cultures tell stories that follow this arc because it maps to the lived experience of transformation.

In neuroscience terms, the Hero's Journey creates what Uri Hasson's research describes as neural coupling — a synchronization of brain activity between storyteller and listener that extends beyond language regions into areas associated with social cognition, emotional processing, and memory formation (see [The neuroscience of strategic storytelling](The-neuroscience-of-strategic-storytelling)). The chronological coherence of the 12 stages is critical to this effect: Hasson's research demonstrated that scrambled narratives produce significantly less coupling than temporally structured ones.

The three-act structure — departure, initiation, return — also aligns with how the brain encodes episodic memories. Events are stored as narrative sequences, not as isolated data points. A B2B message structured as a feature list is processed analytically and quickly forgotten. A B2B message structured as a journey is processed narratively and remembered.

### Ideal B2B application

The Hero's Journey is best suited for complex enterprise sales with the following characteristics:

- **Long sales cycles** (6–18 months) that unfold across multiple stages.
- **Multiple stakeholders** (5 or more decision-makers) who each need to see themselves in the narrative at different stages.
- **High-stakes transformations** where the buyer is not just purchasing a tool but fundamentally changing how their organization operates.
- **Content-rich go-to-market strategies** that can support 12 distinct narrative stages across blog posts, case studies, webinars, sales enablement materials, and implementation documentation.

### Strengths in B2B

- Provides a complete narrative architecture from awareness to advocacy.
- Mirrors the psychological reality of how organizations adopt change.
- Accommodates the complexity of multi-stakeholder B2B decisions.
- Creates natural content-mapping opportunities across the full funnel.
- Produces emotional engagement, not just analytical understanding.

### Weaknesses in B2B

- Requires significant content investment to execute across all 12 stages.
- Can be too complex for transactional, low-consideration purchases.
- Risks feeling artificially dramatic for mundane or commodity products.
- Assumes a linear journey, whereas B2B buying is often non-linear and recursive.
- Demands internal alignment across marketing, sales, and customer success teams to maintain narrative consistency.

## StoryBrand (Donald Miller)

### Origin and development

The StoryBrand framework was introduced by Donald Miller in his 2017 book *Building a StoryBrand: Clarify Your Message So Customers Will Listen*. Miller, a memoirist and novelist before becoming a marketing consultant, observed that most companies fail at marketing not because their products are bad but because their messaging is confusing. His central insight — drawn from screenwriting principles — is that clarity, not cleverness, drives customer engagement.

Miller's framework is explicitly built on narrative structure (particularly the Hero's Journey and Robert McKee's *Story*), but it simplifies radically. Where the Hero's Journey has 12 stages, StoryBrand has 7 elements. Where the Hero's Journey requires a sophisticated understanding of narrative theory, StoryBrand is designed to be completed in a single workshop.

The framework has achieved significant commercial success, particularly among small and mid-market B2B companies. StoryBrand Certified Guides operate globally, and the StoryBrand BrandScript has become a widely used messaging template. The framework's influence on B2B website copy, in particular, has been substantial.

### The 7 parts in detail

**Part 1: A character.** Every story begins with a character who wants something. In StoryBrand, the character is always the customer — never the brand. The "want" must be clearly defined and externally obvious. Vague aspirations ("grow your business") fail because they do not create narrative tension. Specific, concrete desires ("reduce customer churn by 30% in the next two quarters") succeed because they establish a clear gap between the character's current state and their desired state.

**Part 2: Has a problem.** The character faces a problem that prevents them from getting what they want. Miller distinguishes three levels of problem — external, internal, and philosophical — and argues that most B2B companies address only the external problem while ignoring the other two.

The *external problem* is the tangible, functional challenge: outdated software, manual processes, data silos. The *internal problem* is the emotional frustration the external problem creates: the feeling of being overwhelmed, the fear of looking incompetent, the frustration of wasted effort. The *philosophical problem* is the broader injustice: "It shouldn't be this hard to get accurate reporting" or "Companies shouldn't have to choose between speed and quality."

Miller argues that companies that address all three levels of problem create significantly stronger emotional resonance. The philosophical problem, in particular, creates a sense of righteous cause that elevates the purchase from a transaction to a mission.

The problem also introduces the *villain* — the root cause of the problem. The villain is not a competitor but an abstract antagonist: complexity, fragmentation, outdated methodology, information asymmetry. A well-defined villain gives the buyer something to fight against, which is psychologically more motivating than something to move toward (see [Behavioral economics in B2B decisions](Behavioral-economics-in-B2B-decisions) for the research on loss aversion).

**Part 3: And meets a guide.** The guide is the brand. Miller's most important structural contribution is the insistence that the brand must never position itself as the hero. The hero is the customer. The brand is the guide — the Yoda to the customer's Luke Skywalker, the Gandalf to the customer's Frodo.

The guide demonstrates two qualities: *empathy* (showing that the guide understands the customer's pain) and *authority* (showing that the guide has the competence to help). Empathy without authority is useless sympathy. Authority without empathy is arrogant expertise. Both must be present.

In B2B, empathy is demonstrated through language that mirrors the customer's experience ("We know what it's like to present to the board with data you don't trust"). Authority is demonstrated through credentials, case studies, client logos, and data ("We've helped 200+ SaaS companies reduce churn by an average of 34%").

**Part 4: Who gives them a plan.** The guide provides a simple, clear plan — typically three steps — that shows the customer how to engage. The plan exists to reduce cognitive load. Complex B2B purchases involve enormous uncertainty: procurement processes, implementation timelines, organizational change. The plan makes the path forward feel manageable.

Miller recommends two types of plans: the *process plan* (the literal steps to work with you — "Schedule a consultation. Receive a custom roadmap. Launch in 30 days.") and the *agreement plan* (a set of commitments that reduce anxiety — "No lock-in contracts. Dedicated implementation manager. 99.9% uptime SLA.").

**Part 5: And calls them to action.** The guide issues a clear call to action. Miller distinguishes between *direct calls to action* (explicit requests — "Book a demo," "Start your free trial") and *transitional calls to action* (lower-commitment offers — "Download the guide," "Watch the case study"). Both must be present. A website without a clear direct CTA fails because the customer does not know what to do next. A website with only aggressive direct CTAs fails because not every visitor is ready to buy.

**Part 6: That helps them avoid failure.** The narrative must articulate what happens if the customer does *not* act. This is the stakes. Without stakes, there is no urgency. In B2B, failure can be professional (career risk, missed targets), operational (continued inefficiency, competitive disadvantage), or financial (wasted budget, lost revenue).

Miller is careful to distinguish between articulating failure and manufacturing fear. The goal is not to manipulate but to make the real consequences of inaction explicit. If the consequences are not real, the narrative is dishonest. If the consequences are real but unstated, the narrative lacks urgency.

**Part 7: And ends in a success.** The narrative concludes with a vivid depiction of the customer's transformed state — the "after" picture. This must be specific and concrete, not aspirational and vague. "Better outcomes" is meaningless. "Your team reclaims 12 hours per week, your board gets real-time dashboards instead of month-old spreadsheets, and you personally stop dreading the quarterly review" is meaningful.

### Why StoryBrand works

StoryBrand works because it enforces radical clarity. The framework's primary contribution is not its narrative structure (which is a simplified Hero's Journey) but its discipline: it forces companies to stop talking about themselves and start talking about the customer's problem. This is effective because it aligns with how the brain processes relevance. Information is processed more deeply when it is perceived as personally relevant — a phenomenon documented in self-referential encoding research (Rogers, Kuiper & Kirker, 1977).

StoryBrand also exploits cognitive fluency — the finding that information that is easy to process feels more true and more trustworthy (Alter & Oppenheimer, 2009). By reducing messaging to seven clear elements and insisting on short, jargon-free language, StoryBrand produces communication that is easy to process and therefore more persuasive. This is particularly valuable in B2B, where messaging tends toward technical density and insider jargon.

### Ideal B2B application

StoryBrand is best suited for:

- **Website homepages and landing pages** where clarity and conversion are the primary objectives.
- **Brand positioning** for companies whose existing messaging is unclear, internally inconsistent, or overly technical.
- **Mid-market B2B** with moderate sales cycles (1–6 months) and relatively straightforward buying processes.
- **Companies new to narrative strategy** that need a structured starting point with rapid implementation.

### Strengths in B2B

- Extremely actionable — can be implemented in days, not months.
- Forces customer-centricity at the structural level.
- Produces clear, jargon-free messaging that passes the "grandmother test."
- Well-supported by a practitioner ecosystem (certified guides, workshops, templates).
- Excellent for aligning internal teams on core messaging.

### Weaknesses in B2B

- Can feel formulaic when applied without customization, particularly if competitors use the same framework.
- Lacks the depth to serve as a complete narrative architecture for complex enterprise sales.
- The 7-part structure can oversimplify buying processes that involve multiple decision-makers with conflicting priorities.
- Does not explicitly address the post-purchase experience (implementation, adoption, advocacy).
- The emphasis on simplicity can strip away nuance that technical buyers require.

## Problem–Agitate–Solution (PAS)

### Origin and development

Problem–Agitate–Solution is not a storytelling framework in the narrative sense. It is a copywriting formula — one of the oldest and most enduring in direct-response advertising. Its origins are difficult to attribute to a single creator, as it evolved through the collective practice of direct-response copywriters in the mid-20th century. Dan Kennedy, Gary Halbert, Eugene Schwartz, and other direct-response practitioners used and popularized variations of the formula. It appears in Kennedy's *The Ultimate Sales Letter* (1990) and is referenced extensively in the direct-response copywriting tradition.

The formula's endurance is a testament to its effectiveness. PAS remains the dominant structure for cold emails, landing pages, paid advertisements, and top-of-funnel content across B2B marketing. Its power lies in its simplicity and its alignment with fundamental psychological mechanisms.

### The 3-part structure in detail

**Problem.** The opening identifies a specific, concrete pain point that the reader is currently experiencing. The problem must be stated in the reader's language, not the vendor's. It must be immediately recognizable — the reader should feel a jolt of "that's exactly my situation" within the first sentence.

Effective B2B problem statements are narrow and specific rather than broad and abstract. "Struggling with data management" is too vague. "Your sales team wastes 6 hours every week copying data from your CRM into spreadsheets because the systems don't integrate" is specific enough to trigger recognition in the right audience.

The problem statement serves a filtering function: it signals to the reader that this message is relevant to them. Readers who do not experience the stated problem will self-select out, which is desirable — reaching the wrong audience with the right message is worse than not reaching them at all.

**Agitate.** The middle section intensifies the problem by exploring its consequences, second-order effects, and emotional toll. If the problem statement is a diagnosis, the agitation is the prognosis without treatment.

Agitation works by leveraging *loss aversion* — the well-documented finding that the psychological pain of losing is approximately twice as powerful as the pleasure of gaining (Tversky & Kahneman, 1991). By making the losses associated with the problem vivid and tangible, the agitation creates emotional urgency that the rational problem statement alone cannot produce.

Effective agitation follows a logical escalation: from the immediate symptom to the downstream consequences to the ultimate stakes. For example: "Those 6 hours of manual data entry aren't just tedious — they introduce errors that corrupt your pipeline reporting. Your VP of Sales is making decisions based on data that's 72 hours stale. And every quarter, your forecast accuracy erodes a little more, which means your board is losing confidence in your ability to predict revenue."

The agitation must stop short of catastrophizing. Overblown consequences ("Your company will go bankrupt!") destroy credibility. The agitation should feel like an honest, slightly uncomfortable articulation of what the reader already knows but hasn't fully confronted.

**Solution.** The closing introduces the product or service as the specific remedy to the agitated problem. The solution must directly address the specific problem and consequences described in the preceding sections. A generic solution statement ("Our platform helps companies work smarter") fails because it does not connect to the specific pain that was agitated. A specific solution statement ("Our native CRM integration eliminates manual data entry entirely — your pipeline data updates in real time, your forecasts reflect today's reality, and your VP of Sales finally trusts the numbers") succeeds because each clause directly resolves a specific consequence from the agitation.

### Why PAS works

PAS exploits three psychological mechanisms simultaneously:

1. **Loss aversion.** The agitation phase makes the losses associated with the status quo vivid and emotionally salient, which motivates action more powerfully than describing the gains of the solution.
2. **The contrast effect.** By establishing a vivid negative state (the agitated problem) before introducing the solution, PAS creates a perceptual contrast that makes the solution feel more valuable than it would in isolation (Sherif, Taub & Hovland, 1958).
3. **Cognitive momentum.** The three-part structure creates a psychological progression — problem recognition, emotional escalation, resolution — that mirrors the brain's natural pattern-completion instinct. Once the reader is invested in the problem and feeling the agitation, the desire for resolution becomes a cognitive pull toward the solution.

### Ideal B2B application

PAS is best suited for:

- **Cold email outreach** where the reader must be engaged within the first sentence or the message is deleted.
- **Landing pages** optimized for conversion from a specific traffic source (paid search, social ads).
- **Top-of-funnel advertising** (LinkedIn ads, Google Ads, display) where space is limited and attention is scarce.
- **Demand generation campaigns** targeting prospects who have not yet recognized the severity of their problem.

### Strengths in B2B

- Extremely concise — can be executed in a single paragraph or a full page.
- Drives urgency and emotional engagement quickly.
- Easy to learn and replicate across teams.
- Naturally aligns with performance marketing metrics (click-through rate, conversion rate).
- Works well for narrow, well-defined audience segments.

### Weaknesses in B2B

- Can be perceived as aggressive, manipulative, or fear-mongering if the agitation is overdone.
- Lacks the structural depth for relationship-building, long-cycle nurture, or post-purchase communication.
- Focuses entirely on the problem and offers no vision of transformation beyond the immediate fix.
- Poorly suited for brand-building or thought leadership content.
- Risks creating a negative emotional association with the brand if the reader's primary experience is agitation.
- Ethical risk: the formula works equally well for legitimate problems and manufactured ones, so practitioners must exercise discipline in applying it honestly.

## The Pixar Pitch

### Origin and development

The Pixar Pitch derives from Emma Coats' "22 Rules of Storytelling," a list she published in 2011 based on her experience as a storyboard artist at Pixar Animation Studios. Rule #4 states: "Once upon a time there was ___. Every day, ___. One day ___. Because of that, ___. Because of that, ___. Until finally ___."

This six-sentence structure was not originally intended as a business tool. It was a narrative exercise for animators and screenwriters — a way to distill a complex story into its essential spine. Daniel Pink popularized the structure for business contexts in his 2012 book *To Sell Is Human*, where he reframed it as a pitch format.

The structure's power lies in its constraint. Six sentences are enough to establish a character, a conflict, a causal chain, and a resolution — but not enough to include extraneous detail, tangential features, or self-congratulatory positioning. The constraint forces the storyteller to identify what actually matters.

The Pixar Pitch is related to, but distinct from, the And/But/Therefore (ABT) structure developed by Randy Olson (discussed in the additional frameworks section below). Both enforce narrative discipline through structural constraint, but the Pixar Pitch provides a slightly more detailed scaffold.

### The 6-sentence structure in detail

**"Once upon a time..."** — Establishes the protagonist's world and initial conditions. In B2B: "Once upon a time, mid-market SaaS companies relied on their sales team's intuition to forecast quarterly revenue."

**"Every day..."** — Describes the recurring pattern or struggle within that world. In B2B: "Every day, the VP of Sales would ask each rep for their pipeline estimate, manually compile the numbers in a spreadsheet, and present a forecast that everyone privately knew was unreliable."

**"One day..."** — Introduces the catalyst — the event that disrupts the status quo. In B2B: "One day, the board demanded a forecast accuracy rate above 85%, or the executive team would face a restructuring."

**"Because of that..."** — Describes the first consequence of the catalyst. In B2B: "Because of that, the VP of Sales had to find a system that could produce data-driven forecasts without relying on rep self-reporting."

**"Because of that..."** — Describes the escalating consequence. In B2B: "Because of that, the company evaluated six forecasting platforms and discovered that most of them required 6 months of historical data they didn't have."

**"Until finally..."** — Resolves the tension with a new equilibrium. In B2B: "Until finally, they implemented [Product] — which generates accurate forecasts from 30 days of CRM data — and hit 91% accuracy in their first quarter."

### Why the Pixar Pitch works

The Pixar Pitch works through three mechanisms:

1. **Forced brevity.** The six-sentence constraint eliminates everything that is not essential. In B2B, where messaging tends toward bloat, this discipline is invaluable. If you cannot tell your story in six sentences, you do not yet understand your story well enough.

2. **Causal structure.** The repeated "Because of that..." forces the storyteller to establish causality rather than mere sequence. "And then..." is additive — it places events in order without explaining why one led to the next. "Because of that..." is causal — it creates a logical chain that the listener's brain can follow and predict. Hasson's neural coupling research demonstrates that predictive engagement — the listener's brain anticipating the next narrative beat — produces the strongest synchronization.

3. **The memorability test.** A Pixar Pitch, once written, can be spoken in under 30 seconds. If a salesperson can remember and deliver the pitch in an elevator, a taxi, or a hallway conversation, the core narrative has sufficient clarity. If they cannot, the messaging is too complex. This is a practical test of cognitive fluency — a proxy for whether the narrative will survive the chain of retelling that characterizes B2B word-of-mouth.

### Ideal B2B application

The Pixar Pitch is best suited for:

- **Elevator pitches** where the available time is under 60 seconds.
- **Executive summaries** at the top of proposals, reports, or strategy decks.
- **Internal alignment** — ensuring that every team member tells the same core story.
- **Pitch competitions and investor presentations** where brevity is mandatory.
- **Narrative testing** — using the Pixar Pitch as a diagnostic to evaluate whether a more complex narrative has a coherent spine.

### Strengths in B2B

- Forces extreme clarity and narrative discipline.
- Memorable and easily retold by sales teams, champions, and partners.
- Quick to produce — a useful starting point for any narrative project.
- Excellent diagnostic tool: if you cannot complete the six sentences, your narrative has structural problems.
- Accessible to non-marketers (product managers, engineers, executives).

### Weaknesses in B2B

- Too short to serve as a complete content strategy or narrative architecture.
- Cannot accommodate the complexity of multi-stakeholder enterprise sales.
- Provides no guidance on tone, channel, or audience segmentation.
- Lacks explicit treatment of the guide/mentor role, the villain, or the stakes of inaction.
- Best used as a foundational tool within a larger framework, not as a standalone solution.

## Jobs-to-be-Done (JTBD) narrative

### Origin and development

The Jobs-to-be-Done framework originated in the work of Clayton Christensen at Harvard Business School, most fully articulated in his 2016 book *Competing Against Luck: The Story of Innovation and Customer Choice* (co-authored with Taddy Hall, Karen Dillon, and David Duncan). Christensen argued that customers do not buy products — they "hire" products to do a "job" that arises in their lives. Understanding the job, not the customer's demographics or stated preferences, is the key to innovation and effective marketing.

Tony Ulwick's parallel work on Outcome-Driven Innovation (ODI), published in *What Customers Want* (2005) and later in *Jobs to Be Done: Theory to Practice* (2016), provided a more structured, quantitative methodology for identifying and prioritizing jobs. Where Christensen's approach is qualitative and interview-based, Ulwick's is survey-driven and statistical. Both share the core premise: start with the job, not the product.

The JTBD framework is not strictly a storytelling framework. It is a product strategy and innovation framework that produces a narrative structure as a byproduct. Its narrative output — the "When I... I want to... So I can..." statement — is a compact, customer-centric message format that has been widely adopted in product marketing.

### The narrative structure in detail

The JTBD narrative follows a three-part structure:

**"When I..."** — Defines the *situation* or *context* in which the job arises. This is not a demographic description of the customer but a description of the circumstances that create the need. The same person may have entirely different "jobs" in different situations. A VP of Marketing has one job when preparing for a board meeting (need for credible metrics), a completely different job when onboarding a new team member (need for efficient knowledge transfer), and yet another when evaluating a new channel (need for risk assessment).

**"I want to..."** — Defines the *job* — the progress the customer is trying to make. The job should be expressed in functional, measurable terms: "I want to produce an accurate pipeline forecast in under 10 minutes" rather than "I want better forecasting." The specificity of the job statement determines the utility of the framework. Vague jobs produce vague strategy.

**"So I can..."** — Defines the *desired outcome* — the higher-order benefit the customer achieves when the job is done well. This is where the JTBD framework connects functional progress to emotional and social outcomes: "So I can walk into the board meeting with data I actually trust and be seen as a strategic leader rather than an operational reporter."

### Types of jobs

Christensen and his colleagues identified three types of jobs that buyers are trying to accomplish:

- **Functional jobs** are the practical tasks the buyer needs to complete: generate a report, integrate two systems, automate a workflow. These are the easiest to identify and the most commonly addressed in B2B messaging.
- **Emotional jobs** are the feelings the buyer wants to experience or avoid: confidence, relief from anxiety, the satisfaction of a job well done, freedom from the fear of looking incompetent. These are rarely articulated by buyers but powerfully influence decisions.
- **Social jobs** are about how the buyer wants to be perceived by others: as innovative, as strategic, as a leader, as someone who makes smart decisions. In B2B, where purchases are visible to colleagues, managers, and direct reports, social jobs are often the decisive factor — even though they are almost never stated explicitly.

The most effective JTBD narratives address all three types. A narrative that addresses only the functional job ("Our platform generates reports 50% faster") is informative but not persuasive. A narrative that also addresses the emotional job ("You'll never again stay late on a Sunday night manually pulling numbers for Monday's meeting") and the social job ("Your team will see you as the person who finally fixed the reporting problem") is significantly more compelling.

### Why JTBD works

JTBD works because it is radically customer-centric. Unlike frameworks that begin with the product's capabilities or the company's story, JTBD begins with the customer's situation and desired progress. This alignment produces several advantages:

1. **Product-market fit clarity.** By defining the job precisely, JTBD reveals whether the product actually does the job the customer needs done — or whether the product is solving a different job than the one the market is hiring for.
2. **Competitive framing.** JTBD redefines competition. The real competitor is not another vendor in the same category — it is whatever the customer is currently "hiring" to do the job. If the customer is "hiring" a spreadsheet, the narrative must compete against the spreadsheet, not against enterprise software competitors.
3. **Cross-functional alignment.** The JTBD statement serves as a shared reference point across product, marketing, sales, and customer success. Everyone is working toward the same job.

### Ideal B2B application

JTBD is best suited for:

- **Product marketing** for new features, product launches, or category-creation efforts.
- **Landing pages and feature pages** where the messaging must explain what the product does in the customer's terms.
- **Competitive positioning** where the real competitor is the status quo, not another vendor.
- **Product-led growth** strategies where the product itself must communicate its value without sales intervention.
- **Functional B2B tools** where the buyer's primary motivation is getting a specific job done efficiently.

### Strengths in B2B

- Forces genuine customer-centricity at the strategic level.
- Produces messaging that sounds like the customer's own thoughts.
- Bridges product development and marketing, creating alignment.
- Redefines competition in useful ways (the status quo as competitor).
- Scalable — works for individual features, products, and entire product portfolios.

### Weaknesses in B2B

- Can lack emotional resonance if the practitioner focuses exclusively on functional jobs and neglects emotional and social jobs.
- The framework does not provide narrative structure beyond the three-part statement — it tells you *what* to say but not *how* to structure a story.
- Requires deep customer research (qualitative interviews) to execute well, which many organizations skip.
- The "job" can be defined at multiple levels of abstraction, leading to disagreements about the right level of specificity.
- Does not address the guide/mentor dynamic, the villain, the stakes of inaction, or the post-purchase journey.

## Additional frameworks worth knowing

The five frameworks above are the most widely used in B2B narrative strategy. Several additional frameworks deserve brief coverage for specific use cases.

### AIDA (Attention, Interest, Desire, Action)

AIDA is one of the oldest marketing communication models, attributed to E. St. Elmo Lewis in the late 1890s. It describes a sequential psychological process: capture the prospect's *attention*, build *interest* through relevant information, create *desire* by connecting the product to the prospect's needs, and prompt *action* through a clear call to action.

AIDA is not a storytelling framework — it is a persuasion sequence. It remains useful as a structural checklist for advertisements, email campaigns, and landing pages. Its limitation is that it assumes a linear funnel and does not account for the non-linear, committee-driven nature of modern B2B buying. It is best used as a tactical overlay on individual content pieces rather than as a strategic narrative architecture.

### The Challenger Sale narrative (CEB/Gartner)

The Challenger Sale, developed by Matthew Dixon and Brent Adamson at CEB (now Gartner) and published in *The Challenger Sale* (2011), is a sales methodology that includes a specific narrative structure. The Challenger narrative has three components: *teach* the customer something they didn't know about their business, *tailor* the insight to the customer's specific context, and *take control* of the conversation by guiding the customer toward a specific conclusion.

The Challenger narrative is distinctive because it positions the seller not as a responsive solution provider but as a proactive teacher who reshapes the customer's understanding of their own problem. The narrative begins with a "commercial insight" — a reframing of the customer's situation that creates urgency the customer did not previously feel.

This approach is best suited for sellers competing on insight rather than product features, particularly in commoditized markets where functional differentiation is minimal. Its limitation is that it requires genuinely novel insights — if the "teaching" is self-serving or obvious, the approach backfires. It also requires significant seller skill and cannot be easily templated.

### Situation–Complication–Resolution (SCR)

SCR is McKinsey & Company's structured communication framework, widely used in management consulting for executive presentations and memos. The structure is straightforward: describe the current *situation* (neutral facts), introduce the *complication* (what changed or what's at risk), and propose the *resolution* (recommended action).

SCR is valued for its brevity and its alignment with how senior executives process information. Executives typically want to know: Where are we? What's the problem? What should we do? SCR answers all three in sequence, without narrative embellishment.

SCR is best suited for internal business cases, executive briefings, board presentations, and any context where the audience is senior and time-constrained. Its limitation is that it lacks emotional engagement — it is efficient but not memorable, which makes it poorly suited for marketing content or sales narratives intended to build lasting associations.

### And/But/Therefore (ABT)

ABT was developed by Randy Olson, a marine biologist turned filmmaker, and published in *Houston, We Have a Narrative: Why Science Needs Story* (2015). Olson argued that most professional communication suffers from one of two structural problems: it is either a string of disconnected facts connected by "and" (what Olson calls the "And, And, And" pattern) or a random collection of ideas with no connective tissue (the "Despite, However, Meanwhile" pattern).

The ABT template provides narrative structure in a single sentence: "[Established context], **and** [further context], **but** [complication], **therefore** [consequence or resolution]." For example: "Mid-market SaaS companies need accurate revenue forecasts, and most rely on their sales team's self-reported pipeline estimates, but these estimates are consistently inaccurate by 20–40%, therefore companies are making strategic decisions based on data they know is wrong."

ABT is best suited for science communication, grant proposals, research summaries, and any context where the communicator must transform factual content into a compelling narrative. Its strength is that it introduces narrative tension (the "but") into content that would otherwise be flat and informational. Its limitation is that it is a single-sentence structure and cannot serve as a complete narrative framework.

## Decision matrix

The following table maps key decision criteria to the most appropriate framework. In practice, most B2B organizations benefit from combining frameworks — for example, using the Pixar Pitch for internal alignment, StoryBrand for website architecture, the Hero's Journey for long-cycle content strategy, and PAS for outbound email campaigns.

| Criterion | Hero's Journey | StoryBrand | PAS | Pixar Pitch | JTBD |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Deal complexity** | Complex (6–18 months, 5+ stakeholders) | Moderate (1–6 months) | Simple (transactional) | Any (alignment tool) | Moderate to complex |
| **Market maturity** | Established markets with educated buyers | Any | Early-stage or competitive | Any | New categories or underserved jobs |
| **Primary objective** | Full-funnel narrative architecture | Website clarity, brand positioning | Attention capture, urgency creation | Internal alignment, pitch clarity | Product-market fit, feature messaging |
| **Buyer psychology emphasis** | Transformation, identity shift | Clarity, cognitive ease | Loss aversion, urgency | Memorability, simplicity | Progress motivation, job completion |
| **Content volume required** | High (12+ content pieces) | Moderate (website + key collateral) | Low (individual content pieces) | Minimal (1 statement) | Moderate (per job/feature) |
| **Implementation difficulty** | High — requires narrative expertise | Low to moderate — workshop-friendly | Low — easy to learn and replicate | Low — single exercise | Moderate — requires customer research |
| **Product nature** | Transformative platforms, enterprise solutions | Any B2B product or service | Pain-point-specific tools | Any | Functional tools, specific-use solutions |
| **Risk of misuse** | Over-dramatization of mundane products | Formulaic, cookie-cutter messaging | Perceived aggression, fear-mongering | Oversimplification | Emotional flatness |

## How the ALIGN framework uses these

The [ALIGN framework](The-ALIGN-framework) is not itself a storytelling framework. It is the methodology that helps practitioners select, customize, and deploy the right storytelling framework — or combination of frameworks — for a specific business context.

ALIGN's relationship to storytelling frameworks is architectural, not competitive. The frameworks described on this page are the building materials. ALIGN is the blueprint and the construction process.

Specifically:

- **[Phase 1: Assess](Phase-1-Assess)** uses the narrative audit to understand the buyer's decision journey, which reveals which framework's structure most closely matches the buyer's actual experience. If buyers describe a long, multi-stage transformation, the Hero's Journey is the natural fit. If buyers describe a single functional job they needed done, JTBD is more appropriate.

- **[Phase 2: Link](Phase-2-Link)** produces the Strategic Narrative Blueprint™, which is framework-agnostic — it captures the core narrative elements (villain, guide, transformation) that appear across all frameworks.

- **[Phase 3: Integrate](Phase-3-Integrate)** contains the framework selection diagnostic. This is where the practitioner uses the decision matrix above — combined with the insights from Phase 1 and the blueprint from Phase 2 — to select the primary framework and any supporting frameworks for the implementation. The [framework comparison matrix](https://github.com/diShine-digital-agency/align-framework/blob/main/guides/framework-comparison-matrix.md) in the main repository serves as the reference document for this step.

- **[Phase 4: Generate](Phase-4-Generate)** applies the selected framework to produce content across channels. The framework provides the structure; the blueprint provides the content; Phase 4 brings them together.

- **[Phase 5: Navigate](Phase-5-Navigate)** measures whether the deployed framework is producing the intended narrative effects and adjusts the selection as needed based on performance data.

For the neuroscience underlying why narrative structures work at all — regardless of which framework is used — see [The neuroscience of strategic storytelling](The-neuroscience-of-strategic-storytelling). For how cognitive biases influence which framework is most effective for a given audience, see [Behavioral economics in B2B decisions](Behavioral-economics-in-B2B-decisions).
