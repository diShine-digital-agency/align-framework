# Phase 3: Integrate — framework selection and customization

Phase 3 is the architectural bridge between the Strategic Narrative Blueprint™ produced in [Phase 2: Link](Phase-2-Link) and the content production that begins in [Phase 4: Generate](Phase-4-Generate). Where Phase 2 defines *what* the narrative says and *why*, Phase 3 determines *how* that narrative is structurally organized — which storytelling framework will carry the story, how the blueprint elements map to that framework's beats, and how the entire narrative condenses into a repeatable format every employee can use.

This is an architectural decision, not a creative exercise. The framework you choose dictates the cognitive load placed on buyers, the emotional levers available during the sales cycle, and the structural coherence of every content asset produced downstream. Choosing the wrong framework does not produce bad writing — it produces strategically misaligned writing that works against the buyer's decision-making process.

---

## Purpose

The Strategic Narrative Blueprint™ defines the raw strategic elements: the customer transformation journey, the villain, the guide, and the unique mechanism. But a blueprint is not a deployable story. It is a set of strategic components that need to be arranged into a narrative structure — a sequence of beats that a buyer encounters over time, across touchpoints, in an order that moves them from awareness to decision.

Phase 3 exists to make three decisions:

1. **Which storytelling framework fits your business context.** Not all frameworks work for all situations. A transactional SaaS product with a short sales cycle requires a different narrative structure than an enterprise platform selling into a 12-month procurement process. The framework selection diagnostic (Step 3.1) uses a scoring matrix to match context to structure.
2. **How the blueprint maps to the chosen framework.** Each framework has its own structural beats — character roles, plot points, rhetorical moves. The customization mapping (Step 3.2) translates your specific blueprint components into the specific positions within the chosen framework.
3. **How the entire narrative compresses into a single repeatable pitch.** The Pixar Pitch synthesis (Step 3.3) distills the full blueprint into six sentences that any employee — from the CEO to a customer success manager — can memorize and deliver consistently.

Without Phase 3, teams jump from the blueprint directly to content creation, and the choice of narrative structure is made implicitly by whoever writes the first draft. This produces inconsistency: the homepage tells a StoryBrand story, the sales deck follows the Hero's Journey, and email campaigns default to PAS — not because anyone decided this, but because different writers made different instinctive choices. Phase 3 makes the structural decision explicit, documented, and shared.

For a detailed comparison of the storytelling frameworks referenced throughout this phase, see [Storytelling frameworks compared](Storytelling-frameworks-compared).

## Step 3.1: The framework selection diagnostic

The framework selection diagnostic is a structured scoring process that evaluates your business context across five dimensions. Each dimension is scored from 1 to 5, producing a total score between 5 and 25. The total score maps to a recommended framework category.

The full scoring template is available in the repository: [03-integrate-framework-selection.md](https://github.com/diShine-digital-agency/align-framework/blob/main/templates/03-integrate-framework-selection.md).

### The five dimensions

#### Dimension 1: Deal complexity (1–5)

This dimension measures the structural complexity of the purchase decision.

| Score | Description |
| :--- | :--- |
| **1** | Simple transactional purchase. Single decision-maker, short cycle (days to weeks), low price point. |
| **2** | Low complexity. One or two stakeholders, evaluation cycle under 30 days. |
| **3** | Moderate complexity. Small buying committee (3–5 people), 1–3 month cycle. |
| **4** | High complexity. Formal buying committee, 3–6 month cycle, budget approval required. |
| **5** | Enterprise complexity. Multi-stakeholder procurement, 6–18 month cycle, RFP process, legal review. |

Higher complexity scores favor frameworks that support extended narrative arcs with multiple touchpoints (Hero's Journey). Lower scores favor frameworks that deliver clarity and resolution quickly (StoryBrand, PAS).

#### Dimension 2: Market maturity (1–5)

This dimension measures whether you are competing in an established category or creating a new one.

| Score | Description |
| :--- | :--- |
| **1** | Mature, well-defined category. Buyers know exactly what they need and compare vendors on features. |
| **2** | Established category with some differentiation opportunities. |
| **3** | Evolving category. Buyers understand the problem but are uncertain about the best approach. |
| **4** | Emerging category. Buyers may not yet recognize the problem or know that solutions exist. |
| **5** | Category creation. You are defining a new way of thinking about an existing problem. No established vendor landscape. |

Category creation requires a narrative that first establishes *why* the old way is broken before introducing the solution — a structure the Hero's Journey handles well. Mature categories where buyers already understand the problem benefit from the clarity and speed of StoryBrand or PAS.

#### Dimension 3: Primary objective (1–5)

This dimension captures the immediate strategic goal of the narrative.

| Score | Description |
| :--- | :--- |
| **1** | Direct lead generation. The narrative must drive a specific conversion action (demo request, trial signup). |
| **2** | Lead generation with nurture. Conversion is the goal, but the buyer needs education first. |
| **3** | Balanced. Equal weight on lead generation and thought leadership. |
| **4** | Thought leadership with conversion. Positioning as a category authority is the primary goal, with lead generation as a secondary outcome. |
| **5** | Pure thought leadership. The narrative establishes the company as the defining voice in a space. Conversion is a long-term byproduct. |

Lead generation objectives favor frameworks with clear calls to action and short resolution arcs (StoryBrand, PAS). Thought leadership objectives favor frameworks that build a world view over time (Hero's Journey).

#### Dimension 4: Buyer psychology (1–5)

This dimension identifies the dominant psychological driver of the target buyer.

| Score | Description |
| :--- | :--- |
| **1** | Efficiency-driven. The buyer wants to save time, reduce cost, or eliminate friction. The decision is primarily rational. |
| **2** | Efficiency-dominant with some emotional factors (team morale, workload frustration). |
| **3** | Mixed. Both rational efficiency and emotional risk factors play significant roles. |
| **4** | Risk-dominant with some efficiency factors. The buyer is primarily motivated by avoiding negative outcomes (career risk, compliance failure, competitive threat). |
| **5** | Risk and transformation-driven. The buyer seeks fundamental change and is motivated by fear of falling behind, organizational survival, or strategic repositioning. |

Efficiency-driven buyers respond to clear, concise frameworks that demonstrate value quickly. Risk-driven and transformation-driven buyers require narratives that validate their fears, build trust over time, and demonstrate understanding of the stakes — structures the Hero's Journey supports through its extended arc.

For more on the psychological drivers that influence B2B purchase decisions, see [Behavioral economics in B2B decisions](Behavioral-economics-in-B2B-decisions).

#### Dimension 5: Product nature (1–5)

This dimension evaluates what you are selling.

| Score | Description |
| :--- | :--- |
| **1** | Point solution. A single tool that solves a specific, well-defined problem. |
| **2** | Multi-feature tool. Solves several related problems within a defined scope. |
| **3** | Platform. An integrated system that serves multiple use cases or user types. |
| **4** | Methodology or approach. You are selling a way of working, not just a tool. The product is one component of a broader transformation. |
| **5** | Transformation. You are selling an outcome that requires organizational change. The product is the enabler, but the real deliverable is a new operating model. |

Point solutions are best served by frameworks that highlight a specific problem-solution pair (StoryBrand, PAS). Methodology and transformation offerings require narratives that take the buyer through a journey of realization and change — the core structure of the Hero's Journey.

### Score interpretation

Sum the scores across all five dimensions. The total falls between 5 and 25.

| Total score | Recommended framework | Rationale |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **5–11** | StoryBrand or PAS | Your context favors clarity, speed, and direct conversion. Buyers know what they need, the decision is relatively simple, and the narrative must deliver a clear value proposition quickly. |
| **12–18** | Hybrid architecture | Your context has mixed signals. Some dimensions favor simplicity and others favor depth. A hybrid approach uses StoryBrand as the primary structure for conversion-oriented assets and Hero's Journey elements for thought leadership and long-form content. |
| **19–25** | Hero's Journey | Your context involves complex decisions, emerging or new categories, risk-driven buyers, and transformational offerings. The narrative must build a world view, establish trust over an extended arc, and guide the buyer through psychological stages of change. |

The boundaries between these ranges are not absolute. A score of 12 does not mandate a hybrid approach if four of the five dimensions clearly point to StoryBrand. Use the dimensional breakdown to identify which dimensions are driving the score and apply judgment accordingly.

### Owner

The framework selection diagnostic should be owned by the **Head of Content** or **Brand Strategist**. The decision should be reviewed with the leadership team, as it directly affects how the company presents itself across all channels. Do not delegate this to individual content creators — the framework choice must be consistent across the organization.

## Step 3.2: Framework customization mapping

Once the framework is selected, the blueprint components from [Phase 2: Link](Phase-2-Link) must be mapped into the structural beats of the chosen framework. This step translates strategic architecture into narrative architecture.

The mapping process is documented in the framework comparison matrix: [framework-comparison-matrix.md](https://github.com/diShine-digital-agency/align-framework/blob/main/guides/framework-comparison-matrix.md).

### StoryBrand mapping

StoryBrand organizes the narrative into seven parts. When your score falls in the 5–11 range, or when you are producing conversion-focused assets within a hybrid architecture, map the blueprint components as follows:

| StoryBrand element | Blueprint component | Purpose |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **1. A character (the hero)** | Customer transformation journey — before state | Establish who the buyer is and what they want. The buyer is always the hero, never the product. |
| **2. Has a problem** | Villain definition — external, internal, and philosophical problem | Define what stands between the buyer and what they want. Include all three problem levels. |
| **3. And meets a guide** | Guide positioning — empathy and authority statements | Position your brand as the experienced guide. Lead with empathy (you understand their pain), then establish authority (you have the methodology to solve it). |
| **4. Who gives them a plan** | Unique mechanism articulation | Show the buyer a clear, credible path forward. The plan must be specific enough to build confidence and simple enough to remember. |
| **5. And calls them to action** | Primary call to action derived from strategic objectives | Define the one thing you want the buyer to do next. StoryBrand requires a clear, direct CTA — not a menu of options. |
| **6. That helps them avoid failure** | Cost of inaction (from villain definition) | Paint the consequences of not acting. This leverages loss aversion — the psychological reality that the pain of losing is roughly twice as powerful as the pleasure of gaining. |
| **7. And ends in success** | Customer transformation journey — after state | Describe the transformed state: external success (measurable outcomes), internal success (how the buyer feels), and philosophical success (the new reality). |

StoryBrand's strength is clarity. It reduces cognitive load by organizing the narrative into a predictable structure that the buyer can process quickly. This makes it effective for homepages, landing pages, short-form sales content, and any context where the buyer's attention span is limited.

Its limitation is depth. StoryBrand compresses the narrative into a single linear sequence, which limits its ability to handle the extended psychological arc of complex enterprise decisions. For complex sales, the Hero's Journey provides more structural room.

### Hero's Journey mapping

The Hero's Journey organizes the narrative into stages that mirror the psychological process of organizational change. When your score falls in the 19–25 range, or when producing thought leadership and long-form content within a hybrid architecture, map the blueprint to these six critical stages:

| Hero's Journey stage | Blueprint component | Purpose |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **1. The ordinary world** | Customer transformation journey — before state (expanded) | Describe the buyer's current reality in detail: daily frustrations, systemic limitations, the workarounds they have accepted as normal. This stage establishes recognition — the buyer should see their own situation described accurately. |
| **2. The call to adventure** | Trigger event and cost of inaction | The moment the buyer realizes the status quo is no longer sustainable. Something changes — a competitive threat, a failed initiative, a leadership mandate — that forces them to consider a new approach. |
| **3. Meeting the mentor** | Guide positioning — empathy, authority, and unique mechanism | The buyer encounters your brand as the experienced mentor who understands the problem at a deeper level. The guide does not sell a product — they offer a philosophy, a methodology, a way of seeing the problem that the buyer had not considered. |
| **4. Crossing the threshold** | Paradigm shift and unique mechanism (detailed) | The buyer commits to a new approach. This is the point of no return — they have accepted that the old way does not work and have adopted your framing of the problem. The unique mechanism is the vehicle for this crossing. |
| **5. The road of trials** | Implementation journey and proof points | The buyer faces challenges during adoption. Case studies, testimonials, and evidence of others who have successfully made the same journey serve as reassurance during this stage. |
| **6. The return with the elixir** | Customer transformation journey — after state (expanded) | The buyer has completed the transformation and achieved the promised outcomes. They are now equipped to advocate for the change within their organization and beyond. |

The Hero's Journey's strength is psychological fidelity. Complex B2B purchases are not instantaneous decisions — they are processes of organizational change that unfold over months. The Hero's Journey mirrors the actual psychological stages buyers go through: recognition, consideration, commitment, struggle, and transformation. This makes it effective for long-form thought leadership, webinar series, extended sales cycles, and any context where the buyer needs to be guided through a process of realization.

Its limitation is length. The Hero's Journey requires more content, more touchpoints, and more time. For transactional sales or conversion-focused landing pages, it introduces unnecessary complexity.

### Hybrid architecture

Scores in the 12–18 range indicate a context where neither pure StoryBrand nor pure Hero's Journey is optimal. Hybrid architecture uses both frameworks strategically, assigning each to the content types and journey stages where it performs best:

- **StoryBrand** for conversion-focused assets: homepage, landing pages, email CTAs, sales one-pagers, product pages. These assets need clarity, speed, and a direct path to action.
- **Hero's Journey** for depth-focused assets: thought leadership articles, webinar series, keynote presentations, comprehensive guides, case studies. These assets need extended arcs, psychological depth, and room to build a world view.
- **Shared foundation:** both structures draw from the same Strategic Narrative Blueprint™. The villain, the guide, the transformation, and the unique mechanism are identical. Only the structural arrangement changes.

The hybrid approach requires disciplined content tagging. Every content asset must be explicitly tagged with the framework it uses, so that editorial teams maintain consistency within each framework track. Without this discipline, the hybrid becomes an excuse for inconsistency rather than a strategic choice.

## Step 3.3: The Pixar Pitch synthesis

The Pixar Pitch is a six-sentence narrative compression that distills the entire Strategic Narrative Blueprint™ into a format any employee can memorize and deliver. It is named after the story structure used internally at Pixar Animation Studios to pitch film concepts.

The Pixar Pitch is not a marketing tagline. It is not an elevator pitch optimized for cleverness. It is a structural compression of the complete narrative arc — beginning, middle, and end — in six sentences that follow a fixed grammatical pattern.

### The structure

| Sentence | Opening phrase | Blueprint component | Purpose |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| 1 | "Once upon a time..." | Before state | Establish the hero's ordinary world — the buyer's current reality before encountering the problem. |
| 2 | "Every day..." | Daily pain and status quo | Describe the recurring frustration, limitation, or inefficiency the buyer has accepted as normal. |
| 3 | "One day..." | Trigger event / call to adventure | The moment something changes and the buyer realizes the status quo is unsustainable. |
| 4 | "Because of that..." | First consequence / guide introduction | The buyer seeks a new approach and encounters the guide (your brand) and the paradigm shift. |
| 5 | "Because of that..." | Unique mechanism and commitment | The buyer adopts the new approach (your methodology or product) and begins the transformation. |
| 6 | "Until finally..." | After state and transformation | The buyer achieves the promised transformation — external, internal, and philosophical success. |

### Why the Pixar Pitch matters

Three problems the Pixar Pitch solves:

1. **Internal alignment.** When 50 employees describe the company differently to prospects, the market receives 50 fragmented signals. The Pixar Pitch gives everyone the same story in a format that is easy to remember because it follows a natural narrative arc.
2. **Executive communication.** Board presentations, investor pitches, and partner meetings require a concise story. The Pixar Pitch provides this without sacrificing the strategic structure of the full blueprint.
3. **Narrative testing.** If the six sentences do not flow naturally or feel forced, the underlying blueprint likely has structural gaps. The Pixar Pitch functions as a quality check on the blueprint itself.

### Owner

The Pixar Pitch should be drafted by the same person or team that owns the Strategic Narrative Blueprint™ — typically the **Head of Marketing** or **Brand Strategist**. It must be reviewed and approved by the CEO and sales leadership, because they are the people who will use it most frequently in unscripted conversations.

## Why the framework choice matters methodologically

The framework selection in Step 3.1 is not a matter of personal preference or aesthetic taste. Different narrative structures activate different cognitive processes in the audience, and the mismatch between structure and context produces measurable inefficiency.

### Cognitive load and decision speed

StoryBrand and PAS are structurally simple. They present a problem, introduce a solution, and resolve with a clear outcome. This structure reduces cognitive load — the mental effort required to process and evaluate the message. For fast decisions where the buyer already understands the problem and is comparing solutions, low cognitive load is an advantage. The buyer can process the narrative quickly, identify relevance, and take action.

The Hero's Journey is structurally complex. It introduces multiple stages, extends the narrative arc, and asks the buyer to follow a transformation process over time. This increases cognitive load. For fast, transactional decisions, this additional load creates friction that slows conversion.

However, for complex enterprise decisions involving organizational change, higher cognitive load is appropriate and necessary. The buyer is not making a quick feature comparison — they are evaluating whether to adopt a new way of working. The Hero's Journey mirrors the psychological stages of this evaluation process: recognition of the problem, commitment to change, struggle during implementation, and eventual transformation. Reducing this to a simple problem-solution pair trivializes the decision and undermines trust.

### Emotional processing depth

StoryBrand activates what behavioral researchers call System 1 processing — fast, intuitive, emotionally immediate. It works with the buyer's existing mental models and presents information in a familiar structure. This is effective when the emotional case is straightforward (save time, reduce cost, avoid hassle).

The Hero's Journey activates System 2 processing — slower, deliberate, deeply evaluative. It asks the buyer to reconsider their existing assumptions, adopt a new frame of reference, and commit to a process of change. This is necessary when the emotional case involves complex factors: career risk, organizational transformation, strategic repositioning, or the fear of being left behind by industry shifts.

Choosing StoryBrand for a complex transformation sale is not just a stylistic mistake — it fails to engage the deeper cognitive processes the buyer needs to complete before making a commitment of that magnitude. Choosing the Hero's Journey for a simple transactional sale is not just verbose — it introduces psychological complexity that makes the buyer question whether the decision is more difficult than it actually is.

For a deeper treatment of the neuroscience behind narrative processing, see [The neuroscience of strategic storytelling](The-neuroscience-of-strategic-storytelling).

## Common mistakes

### Choosing a framework based on personal preference

The most frequent error. A marketing leader reads *Building a StoryBrand* and decides the company should use StoryBrand — without evaluating whether StoryBrand fits the company's deal complexity, market maturity, or buyer psychology. The framework selection must be driven by the diagnostic scoring in Step 3.1, not by which book was most recently read.

### Using the Hero's Journey for transactional sales

The Hero's Journey is designed for extended narrative arcs. Applying it to a $50/month SaaS tool with a self-serve signup flow introduces unnecessary complexity. The buyer does not need to go on a psychological transformation journey to decide whether to try a task management app. StoryBrand or PAS delivers the message faster and more effectively in this context.

### Using StoryBrand for category creation

StoryBrand assumes the buyer already understands the problem and is looking for a solution. If you are creating a new category — defining a problem the buyer does not yet recognize — StoryBrand cannot carry the weight of the paradigm shift. Category creation requires a narrative that first establishes *why* the old way is broken, a structure the Hero's Journey supports through its "ordinary world" and "call to adventure" stages.

### Forgetting the Pixar Pitch

Teams complete the framework selection and customization mapping but skip the Pixar Pitch synthesis, assuming it is optional. It is not. Without the Pixar Pitch, the narrative exists in a long-form document that only the marketing team reads. Sales, customer success, executives, and partners need a compressed version they can deliver from memory. Skipping the Pixar Pitch means the narrative stays locked in a strategy document instead of being deployed across every conversation.

### Applying one framework uniformly across all content

Even when the scoring clearly recommends one framework, not every content asset benefits from the same structure. A company whose score indicates Hero's Journey still needs StoryBrand-style clarity on its pricing page. A company whose score indicates StoryBrand still benefits from Hero's Journey depth in its annual industry report. The hybrid architecture model in Step 3.2 exists precisely for this reason. The framework recommendation is a *default*, not an absolute rule.

## Outputs of Phase 3

Phase 3 produces the following deliverables:

- **Framework selection decision** — documented with the scoring matrix and dimensional rationale.
- **Customization mapping** — the Strategic Narrative Blueprint™ components mapped to the specific structural beats of the chosen framework (StoryBrand 7-part structure, Hero's Journey 6-stage arc, or hybrid).
- **Pixar Pitch** — six sentences capturing the full narrative arc in a memorizable format.

These outputs feed directly into [Phase 4: Generate](Phase-4-Generate), where the narrative architecture is translated into specific content assets across channels and buyer journey stages. Without Phase 3, Phase 4 lacks structural guidance — content creators must make their own framework decisions, producing the inconsistency that the ALIGN methodology exists to prevent.
